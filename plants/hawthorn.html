Hawthorn

Botanical Name

Crataegus arter

Body Systems Affected

Cardiovascular System

Common Names

English: Hawthorn

Danish: Hvidtjørn

French: Aubépine

Habitat and Growing Conditions

Habitat

Geographic: Native to southern Scandinavia, the Baltic region, and other parts of Europe.

Location: Found in woodlands, hedges, thickets, and disturbed or semi-cleared areas.

Elevation: Can grow from sea level up to 1,800 m altitude.

Soil: Grows on a variety of soils, including limestone, granite, or volcanic rock, and is adaptable to chalk, clay, loam, and sand. Avoid areas with constantly waterlogged soil.

Growing Conditions

Sunlight: Prefers full sun to partial shade. It can grow in shade but may have poor growth and pale leaves in very low light.

Soil pH: Tolerates a range of soil pH levels, including acid, alkaline, or neutral.

Water: Has average water needs. It is drought-tolerant once established but needs regular watering during its first year to develop a strong root system.

Hardiness: Very hardy, tolerating cold temperatures. Can withstand exposed conditions and urban pollution.

Pruning: Requires minimal pruning, typically just for removing dead, diseased, or misplaced branches. Pruning is best done in late winter.

Harvesting

Leaves: Although leaves can be harvested in spring, summer, and autumn, they are much more beautiful and tender in spring when they are just emerging.

Flowers: Harvest flowers when barely open or still in bud, otherwise the petals will fall off during drying. Dry them away from sunlight in a dry place. Store in airtight jars to preserve them as long as possible away from moisture.

Fruits: Harvest the fruits in autumn when they are still a beautiful red color. They will keep their nice red color which will darken slightly, and will wrinkle slightly while hardening.

Active Constituents

Flavonoids: These are a major group of beneficial compounds found in hawthorn leaves, flowers, and fruits (include hyperoside, rutin, isoquercetin, and vitexin).

Proanthocyanidins: Also known as OPCs, these are a type of flavonoid that are particularly important for cardiovascular health. They are composed of smaller units like catechin and epicatechin.

Organic Acids: Hawthorn is rich in various organic acids (include malic, citric, and ascorbic acid).

Triterpenes: Ursolic and oleanolic acid, found in hawthorn flowers and leaves.

Pectin: Hawthorn fruit is a source of pectin, a type of soluble fiber.

Properties

Antiarrhythmic, Antioxidant, Blood pressure regulating, Cardioprotective, Heart tonic, Vasodilator

Uses

Hawthorn is the most well-known heart plant, the most respected, and probably the one that brings the most results in a wide variety of cases.
