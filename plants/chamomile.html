Chamomile

Botanical Name

Anthemis nobilis/Chamaemelum nobile

Body Systems Affected

Nervous System Immune System Digestive System

Common Names

English: Chamomile

Danish: Romersk kamille

French: Camomille romaine

Habitat and Growing Conditions

Growing Conditions

Perennial: This type is a long-lived plant that will come back each year from its root system.

Sunlight: Full sun to partial shade. Full sun is ideal for blooming.

Soil: Prefers well-drained, loamy, or sandy soil with a neutral pH. It does not need rich soil, and too much fertilizer can decrease flower quality.

Water: Keep the soil moist but not soggy, especially when seedlings are young. Established plants are drought-tolerant and only need watering during prolonged dry periods.

Planting and Harvesting

Planting: Sow seeds directly in the garden in May, or start them indoors 6-8 weeks before the last frost and plant out in June.

Propagation: It's best to propagate new plants by dividing established clumps in the spring or fall, rather than sowing seeds.

Sowing: Since seeds need light to germinate, surface sow them and press them lightly into the soil without covering them.

Transplanting: Handle seedlings gently, as they do not transplant well once they get larger. Consider planting the entire small container the seedling is in if possible.

Harvesting: Harvest the flowers in July and August.

  • When: Pick on a dry morning, just after the dew has dried, to capture the peak essential oils.
  • How: Cut the flower heads when they are fully open. You can also snip branches with open flowers.
  • Drying: Hang bunches of branches to dry in a cool, well-ventilated area. Once dry, you can strip the blossoms from the stems and store them in an airtight container.

Active Constituents

Flavonoids (like apigenin, luteolin, and quercetin)

Essential oil components (such as chamazulene and bisabolol)

Sesquiterpene lactones (like nobilin)

Properties

Antiallergic, Anti-inflammatory (general), Anti-inflammatory (digestive), Anti-ulcer, Antirheumatic, Antioxidant, Astringent, Sedative, Bitter tonic, Carminative, Cholagogue, Vulnerary, Hypnotic, Immunomodulating, Antispasmodic, Analgesic, Antifungal

Uses

Chamomile is used for the digestive system and the nervous system. It can be used for migraines, especially if the migraines are due to digestive or nervous system issues. Externally, it can be used for the eyes (tiredness or conjunctivitis, for example).

Side Effects

Caution: Be careful if you are allergic to the Asteraceae family (daisy family), it's best to avoid this plant.

Pertinent Information

Family

Asteraceae (Daisy family)

Parts Used

The flowers

Forms Used and Doses

Infusion: 5 to 10 flower heads per cup (Valnet). Ideally taken before meals, as it is a bitter plant that will prepare the digestion. If the infusion is too bitter for some people, then take tincture instead.

Tincture: Use 500 ml of 45° alcohol for 100 g of dried flowers. Note: You will likely need to place a clean stone on the flowers to weigh them down so the alcohol covers them well in the jar. Dosage: 40 to 60 drops per dose, in a little water. Multiple doses per day can be taken if necessary.

Herbal Oil: Can be used externally for all types of skin issues.
